In a recent speech, Al  Orter, a four-time gold medalist in the discus, told of how an Olympic coach wrote to him advising him to quit weight training as it would hinder his discus throwing ability. ## BENEFITS  OF  RESISTANCE TRAINING

## Muscular Strength

Muscle strength, defined as the maximum force generated by a muscle or group of muscles, is an important functional ability for an athlete. Maximum strength of a muscle is dependent on cross-sectional area, muscle length, fiber-type composition, and velocity of movement (16.43). An  increase in maximal force production  is the most common adaptation to heavy resistance exercise training (6.23.25.51). Hikkinen et al (36) have shown that a higher pretraining status will  limit the amount of absolute gains in strength observed in highly trained athletes. Thus, the magnitude of gains will  be in part defined by  the extent of adaptations which have already taken place in prior training. Increasing the strength of a muscle or group of muscles may facilitate an improvement in sporting performance.  For example, an increase in sports-specific isometric leg strength is likely to improve the performance  capabilities of a downhill-skiing competitor.

## Neural Involvement

At  the onset of a resistance  training program, there is a rapid improvement in strength which cannot be explained by  increases in muscle mass or fiber size (17,20,47,77-79, 92). Also, increases in muscle mass are considerably less than increases in voluntary strength (61,70,81), indicating nervous system adaptations. Neural  involvement explains the increases in strength observed in  the early phases of training when  muscle protein  accretion cannot account for the rapid strength increases and after very long-term training when size changes in  muscle are minimal (23,77).

The activation of motor units is governed by  the "size principle." This principle is based upon the relationship between motor unit size and the order or pattern of recruitment. Specifically, motor units are recruited according to their size and recruitment thresholds. The small motor units generate the least force and are recruited  first, while larger motor units are recruited  last (8,37). Since most muscles contain a range of motor units  (ie., type I and type I1 fibers), a continuum of force production  capabilities exists.

## Injury and the Elite Athlete

Based on the adaptations which occur in bone, muscle, and connective  tissue following resistance training (81) and the cross-sectional studies comparing highly resistancetrained athletes to untrained controls, it may be postulated that resistance training has the potential to minimize or offset the incidence of injuries common  to the elite athlete. In addition, the physiological adaptations associated with  tissue remodeling may improve the capacity to repair and heal damaged tissue.

Resistance training is a very safe activity if  proper technique and rigorous supervision is employed, even during heavy lifting (40,99). Direct quantification of a cause-andeffect relationship between resistance training and the incidence of injury is not possible because  preexisting  injuries may become manifest with the stress of a workout. Nevertheless, the incidence of injury to athletes in the weight room  is minimal at 0.13 injuries per 1,000 hours of athletic exposure (99). Since most acute injuries are related to improper technique, stringent instruction and supervision are essential. If  proper exercise form is  not maintained, then  the resistance must be lowered. Chronic injuries associated with  weight training include microtrauma to musculoskeletal structures and overuse injuries.

## MORPHOLOGICAL ADAPTATIONS

## Cardiovascular Adaptations

Resistance training can produce many beneficial cardiovascular changes (27). Both animal and human studies indicate that increases in absolute heart weight and left ventricular hypertrophy is accompanied by  a stronger myocardium and increased stroke volume during rest and exercise (42,8587). Septa1  wall and freewall  thicknesses are also greater in weight lifters compared with  nonathletes (22). These adaptations do not appear to be detrimental to normal functioning of the cardiac tissue because competitive weight lifters and power lifters exhibit normal ratios of left ventricular mass to volume dimensions (21,22,24).

## Skeletal Muscle Adaptations

Despite the fact that high forces which activate both high and low threshold  motor units are essential for the growth process to occur (12), little else is known about the exact mechanisms responsible for skeletal muscle hypertrophy (41). Force developed by  the myofilaments may stimulate the uptake of amino acids and thus result  in growth of muscle tissue (26). Another possible stimulus for growth is muscle damage and a subsequent increase in metabolites within the muscle (43). Heavy forces encountered during resistance exercise lead to the disruption of Z disks, and this disorganization  of the infrastructure may cause the myofibrils to split and grow back to full size (28). Increases in muscle size are associated with an increase in protein  synthesis during the repair process, similar to the overcompensation of muscle glycogen which results following endurance training. Although  the stimulus for growth has not been fully elucidated, the increase in muscle size occurs as a result of greater protein  synthesis than protein  d e p dation, thus, increasing the size and number of myofibrils within a muscle fiber  (33,60). Hormonal mechanisms also appear to augment and, in part, mediate the growth process of skeletal  muscle with  resistance exercise training (49.51 ). However, further research is  required to fully understand the growth process.

## Hypertrophy Vs.  Hyperplasia

Resistance exercise is a potent stimulus to increase the size of muscle  (3,30,91). For a muscle to become larger, it must either increase the size (hypertrophy) or number (hyperplasia) of fibers which make up the muscle. Many investigators believe the number of muscle fibers is innate  (ie., the number is fixed at birth  or soon after)  (10) and does not change during life  (65). As a result, any increase in  muscle size results from an increase in  the crosssectional area of the individual fibers due to an increase in the number of myofibrils within a muscle fiber. In contrast, muscle hyperplasia involves an increase in muscle size as a result of an increase in fiber number (32, 57,69,88,9O).  Different views exist regarding the role of hyperplasia. Some researchers believe that hypertrophy does not have to precede hyperplasia (2),  while others hypothesize that hyperplasia and hypertrophy occur simultaneously (18). One theory states that hypertrophy may be a precursor to hyperplasia in which the individual fibers increase to a size where they become metabolically or mechanically inefficient and then split, forming two daughter fibers (31). Increased satellite cell activity with training may stimulate new fiber formation  (5),  which may be reflective  of hyperplasia or an independent event associated with the recovery process after muscle injury or damage. Elite body builders have been shown to exhibit a higher than normal number of muscle fibers, s u p porting the notion  of hyperplasia (62,90). However, this may simply reflect a genetic predisposition of elite body builders toward a higher number of naturally occurring muscle fibers. Hyperplasia critics attribute these findings to histological artifact, inappropriate sampling procedures, and the fact that many of the investigations only studied single or multipennate muscles (29,41) . Although the hypertrophy/hyperplasia  debate continues in the literature (4), our belief is that, if  hyperplasia occurs, it probably accounts for less than 5% of the adaptive strategies in muscle size increases.

## Muscle Fiber-Type Adaptations and Transformations

The cross-sectional area and percentage of fiber types within a muscle will  vary significantly among elite athletes depending on the sport. The possibility exists to modify the characteristics of skeletal muscle if  the stimulus presented is sufficient. Generally, a muscle will  respond to a given load by  increasing the size of the individual muscles fibers activated in the movement  (9,90). Both animal and human studies support the notion  that resistance exercise training tends to preferentially  hypertrophy the more plastic type I1 muscle fibers as opposed to the more malleable type I fibers  (1  7,19,62,94,98).

A great deal of plasticity exists with  regard  to changes in muscle with exercise. This is due in part to the complex yet readily adaptable group of myosin contractile and regulatory proteins. The majority of resistance exercise research has focused upon the myosin molecule and examination of fiber types based upon his- tochemical myosin adenosine triphosphatase  (rnATPase) staining activities at different pHs. Resuits demonstrating shifts from type I to type I1 fibers  (98) and type I1 to type I  (34, 75,76) fibers have been reported with resistance training, thus, continuing this debate  (56). It is generally agreed that weight training results in an increase in the type IIA muscle fiber population with  transitions  moving from type IIB (also called IIX or IID fiber in  the rat) to IIA with resistance exercise training  (55,78,79).

It appears that as soon as a type IIB muscle fiber is stimulated it starts a process of transformation toward the type IIA profile by  changing the quality of proteins and expressing different types and amounts of rnATPase. Thus, following a resistance training program, very few type IIB fibers remain. Muscle fiber-type conversions may occur very early (as early as four workouts) during a resistance training program. Interestingly, this shift from type IIB to type IIA is reversed during detraining. Furthermore, it appears that when resistance training is started again, the conversion from type IIB to type IIA is quicker, relative to starting in an untrained  state. ## Metabolic Adaptations

High intensity, low volume resistance training decreases capillary density (58,89,93),  while low intensity, high volume resistance  training increases capillary density. Heavy resistance training reduces mitochondrial volume density (58-60,64)  and mitochondrial/myofibrillar volume ratio as a result of a preferential  increase in myofibrillar material and a concomitant dilution of mitochondrial volume  (l,58,63). Importantly, the endurance performance capabilities of resistance-trained  athletes are unaltered when endurance training is combined with a conditioning program  (23,55,56). In fact, resistance training may augment endurance performance normally associated with aerobic training (66,74),  in part, due to an upward shift in the lactate threshold  (66).

## Endocrine Responses and Adaptations

Hormones can affect almost every physiological function in the body via diverse mechanisms (52). Cellular transport, enzyme synthesis, cell growth, protein synthesis, cell metab olism, and reproductive function are a few physiological events which are partially mediated by  hormones. The close association between hormones and the nervous system make the neuroendocrine  system potentially one of the most important physiological systems related  to resistance training adaptations. The major anabolic hormones  (eg., growth  hor- mone, insulin, thyroid hormones, insulin-like growth factors, testosterone) influence normal growth patterns and responses to training  (51, 54). Resistance training can alter the circulating concentrations of  these hormones. For example, resting testosterone concentrations may be increased with  resistance training and decreased with endurance training.

Kraemer et al  (53) determined that acute resistance exercise can increase circulating concentrations of hormones differentially depending on the configuration of the acute program variables (eg., loads, number of sets, rest between sets, etc.). For example, the highest growth hormone, Beta-endorphin, and cortisol concentrations were observed with short rest  (1 minute), 10 R M   (ie., the maximal load that can be lifted for 10 repetitions within one set) multiple-set protocols.  Testosterone a p pears responsive to both a high intensity, long rest  (3  minutes), moderate resistance  (5 RM)  exercise protocol as well  as a  10 R M   short rest protocol. Thus, rest period length and intensity of the exercise session can affect the magnitude of the hormonal response to a workout. The following factors influence the acute hormonal response to a resistance exercise workout  (48) : amount of muscle mass recruited; intensity of the workout; amount of rest between sets and exercises; volume of total work; and training level of the individual.

In addition to anabolic functions, many hormones also help to meet the metabolic demands of strenuous exercise. Regulation of blood glucose, muscle glycogen and triglyceride storage, and mineral metabolism are all mediated via  hormones. The complexity of the neuroendocrine system has just  started to be realized as a myriad of interactions occur among hormones and hormonal factors. Furthermore, endocrine function  is highly integrated with  nutritional  status, nutritional  intake, training status, and other external factors  (eg., stress, sleep, disease) which affect the remodeling and repair processes of the body.

The endocrine system plays a major  role in adaptational  responses of skeletal muscle. Staron et al  (79) demonstrated that serum testosterone concentrations increased after 6 weeks of training and then returned to pretraining values. During this time, significant changes in the quality of protein  took place, emphasizing the role of hormones in the process of protein synthesis/degradation. The effects of resistance training on acute and chronic hormonal responses require further study. The challenge is to link physiological responses to chronic adaptations such as muscle hypertrophy and strength.

## WEIGHT  TRAINING  PRINCIPLES AND  PROGRAM GUIDELINES FOR THE  ELITE  ATHLETE

Weight training programs for the elite athlete must be individualized to match  the physiological and specific demands of the sport (23,67). Most elite athletes will  have an adequate athletic fitness base necessary to perform an individualized sports-specific training program. Although  an athlete may excel in his/her  particular sport, the training age  (ie., length of time the athlete has been using a resistance in training) must be estab lished so an appropriate periodized training program  can be designed.

## Periodization

To ensure progress is made over a long period of time, some type of periodization  or program variation is required. Periodiiration involves the organization  of training into distinct periods  (macrocycles and mesocycles) with  the aim of maximizing the training adaptations. The principle of program variation within a training program was developed by  Soviet Union and Bulgarian conditioning specialists and is based on the work of the endocrinologist, Hans Selye (67). Periodized  training programs are now commonly performed  by  elite athletes, as these programs have been shown to be superior to those which do not incorporate variation  in  training stimulus  (83,96). The specific aims of each cycle are strategically designed such that the athlete peaks at a critical time point (eg., a major competition)  (95). Variation within a training program  can include alterations in the loading cycles, the exercises used, the intensity of the training sessions, and the volume  (set and repetitions)  of the exercises performed. A periodized weight training program should be designed by  the conditioning specialist before the beginning of the competitive season yet be open to minor modifications depending on the athlete's individual physical responses. The major aims of a welldesigned  periodilation program include avoiding overtraining, providing time for physical and mental  recovery, and allowing the athlete to make continued gains throughout the training period.

In a typical periodization training program, the year is broken into specific training cycles (ie., macrocycle, mesocycle, and microcycle), with each phase having specific goals. The macrocycle is the longest and includes two or three mesocycles. Within a given training year, various objectives are emphasized, and the training programs are designed accordingly with each phase of a cycle emphasizing different motor abilities. Generally, high volume exercises at a low intensity are performed at the beginning of the cycle, with lower volume exercises at higher intensities performed closer to the competition phase of the periodization cycle. Several short cycles are preferred  over fewer large cycles. Thus, the length of each phase is commonly reduced to as low as 2-week cycles (84,96). Generally, a minimum of 6 weeks are allowed for all phases to be completed. A detailed review of periodization  and specific examples of periodization  cycles incorporating general prepreparation, preparation, power, strength, high strength  ( o p tional), transition phase, and active rest phases is  beyond the scope of this article  (see ref.  23).

## Acute Program Variables

All  periodized strength  training programs begin with the develop ment of a basic workout. The variables which need to be considered in the development of a workout include choice of exercise, order of the exercises, number of sets, intensity of the exercise, and duration of rest between sets and exercises.

Where possible, the choice of exercise should be specific to the type of exercises performed  by  the athlete in competition, in relation to movement pattern, movement velocity, and the muscle groups activated. As a result,  the choice of equipment is crucial. Examples related to the choice of exercises related to power development are discussed later.

The order of exercises performed by  the elite athlete typically involves performance of large muscle group exercises prior to smaller muscle group exercises. Since the metabolic demand of large muscle group exercises is substantially larger relative to smaller muscle group exercises, compound exercises (those that recruit more than one muscle group) should be performed before isolation exercises.

For elite athletes, the number of sets employed within a workout is directly related  to individual training goals. Typically, three to six sets are used to achieve maximal strength gains. Multiple-set programs optimize the development of strength and local muscular endurance (6). Gains in strength occur more rapidly with multiple-set programs compared with single-set protocols (68). Singleset exercise programs may be effective for individuals who are untrained or those just  beginning a resistance training program. One-set workouts might also be used for maintenance programs.  Furthermore, few differences are noted between single- and multiple-set programs when compared  over short-term training periods and nonperiodized multiple-set programs. Strength changes may not be different between one, two, or three sets of  10-12  R M   during the first several months of training in untrained individuals when the programs are not periodized.  However, when a single-set protocol is compared with  a multiple-set periodized program, significantly superior results are observed with  the multiple-set periodized program  (23). In more highly trained athletes, strength gains are not achieved with  low volume. single-set training programs  (96). Therefore, the use of one- or two-set programs may be more appropriate for beginners in the initial stages of an introductory program  (first 6-12 workouts) for circuit weight training protocols or short-term in-season p r e grams for athletes.

The intensity of the exercise or the amount of resistance/load  used for a specific exercise is possibly the most important variable in resistance training  (68). The use of repetition maximums (RM, the maximal load that can be lifted a given  number of repetitions within one set) is a common method for determining the load. Research supports the basis for a repetition  maximum continuum (23). This continuum simply relates RM resistances to the broad  training effects derived  from their use. R M resistances of 5 6   have the greatest effect on strength measures or maximal power outputs whereas R M   resistances 120  show the greatest effect on muscular endurance measures. This continuum makes it possible to develop a particular feature of muscular performance  to varying degrees over a range of R M   resistances. Either a training R M   target  (eg., 10 RM) or, more commonly, a RM target zone  (a range such as 3-5  RM) is used during a training cycle. Another method of determining resistances for an exercise involves using a percentage of a particular RM  range  (eg., 70% or 85% of the 1 RM). This method requires that the maximal strength in various lifts used in  the training program  be evaluated regularly. If  1 R M   testing is not estab lished regularly  (eg., each week), the percentage of 1 RM used in training decreases and, thus, the training intensity is reduced. The use of percent 1 R M   resistances is warranted  for lifts related  to the competitive Olympic lifts  (eg., clean  and  jerk,  snatch). Since these lifts require coordinated movements and maximal power development from many muscles to result in correct lifting technique, the movements cannot be performed at a true RM  (ie., to momentary failure). The reduction in velocity and power output experienced in  the last repetition of an R M   set are not conducive for such structural lifts related  to the Olympic competitive lifts (eg., power cleans, snatches, power snatches, hang cleans, etc.). Therefore, the percentage of 1 RM  is warranted  to correctly calculate resistances for such lifts.

Hoeger et al  (38,39) identified the relationship between the percent of  1 R M   and the number of repetitions that can be performed. This relationship appears to vary with  the amount of muscle mass needed to perform  the exercise  (eg., leg press requires more muscle mass than knee extensions). When using machine resistances with 80% of the  1 RM,  it was  previously thought to be primarily a strength-related prescription. However, the number of repetitions that could be performed was typically greater than  10, especially for large muscle group exercises such as the leg press. The larger muscle group exercises appear to need a higher percentage of the 1 RM  to keep them in the 'strength' RM  zone of the repetition  continuum.

To illustrate how use of the percent 1 R M   method can result in less than an optimal resistance to increase strength, imagine a strength coach places a sign on the leg press machine that states "use 80% of  1 RM  for all sets." An  athlete uses 80% of the  1 R M   and performs 20 repetitions. The question arises "will performing 20 repetitions per set result in optimal strength increases?" Based on the R M   continuum, a 20 RM  is primarily related to development of local muscular endurance and not optimal development for strength and power. In general, a certain percentage of the 1 R M   with free-weight exercises will  allow fewer repetitions than the same percentage of  1 R M on a similar exercise performed  on a machine. This is due, most likely, to the need for greater balance  and control in space with  free weights.

One of the more overlooked variables in exercise prescription  has been the duration of the rest periods between sets and exercises. Recently, an understanding of the influence that rest periods have in dictating the stress of the workout and the amount of resistance that can be used has been shown. Kraemer et al  (50,5254) have highlighted  the influence of rest periods on the blood lactate, hormonal concentrations, and metabolic responses to resistance exercise training protocols in both men and women. The amount of work performed and the duration of the force demands placed upon muscle determine the blood lactate concentrations. Short rest periods significantly elevate blood lactate concentrations compared with  longer rest periods. Higher volumes of work  (10 RM) result in  higher blood lactate contractions. Typically, elite athletes in sports such as wrestling,  judo,  and middle distance sprints employ such workouts to enhance their ability to tolerate  the large changes in acidbase status with  their events (eg., lactate concentrations of 19 mmol/L  in wrestling). The use of highly intense workouts must be introduced slowly

(eg., start at 2 minutes of rest and, over 6 weeks, move to  1-minute rest periods) into a training program as toleration of increased muscle and blood acid levels (decreased pH) and the acid-base buffer mechanisms in the body adapt.

Recovery between exercise training sessions is crucial to maximize physiological adaptations to training. Competitive athletes often split a resistance training workout i n t ~ two or more components, thereby allowing longer rest periods between sets and exercises, improved recovery from an exercise session, and enhanced quality of training.  The program can be divided according to muscle size, p e sition, and/or  metabolic demand. The length of time between successive workouts for a particular muscle group will  depend on the type of training imposed, the size of  the activated  muscles, and the intensity at which they were trained. Generally, a minimum of 48 hours of rest is required between  intense workouts that train the same muscle group.

Unlike overtraining, which occurs when there is insufficient recovery time within a training phase, overreaching occurs when an excess of training is scheduled for only a few days within a cycle  (87). The athlete is capable of recovery from this excessive load within a few days compared with an overtrained athlete who may take months or even years to recover. The aim of the overreaching training phase is to improve performance via a rebound effect  (87). Extreme caution should be taken when scheduling an overreaching training phase, as it may  result  in  the primitive stages of overtraining. Due to the minimal amount of work in this area, estab lishing the exact training load for an athlete during an overreaching phase must be based on trial and error.

Identifying the specificity of each weight-training task is essential to ensure that any improvement in size, strength, and/or  power can be translated to an improvement in athletic performance.

Understanding training and testing specificity is also vital  to maximize training adaptations. Inappropriately, many studies train subjects with  "isotonic" muscle actions yet test under isometric or isokinetic modalities. Such determinations of strength changes are not as accurate as the training modality itself  (23). If  an individual trains and is tested  using isometric muscle actions, improvements in strength are likely. Therefore, the design of weight training programs, where possible, should employ the types of muscular actions encountered in the sport.

It is welldocumented  that exercising at a specific velocity increases strength at that velocity without major effects on strength at other velocities  (1  1).  If  direct improvements in sporting performances are sought, the athlete should perform the exercise at the velocity encountered during the actual sporting event. Many sporting events require a high velocity of movement and, thus, training at a fast velocity will  result in gains in strength and power to a greater extent than training at a slow velocity (45). Use of velocity-specific training to maximize strength and power gains at a specific velocity of movement is appropriate for the athlete at some point in their training.

## Development of Explosive Strength and Power With Resistance Training

Explosive resistance training creates specific increases in muscle activation and rates of force develop ment (36). Increases in what is called "explosive strengthn occur when stretchshortening cycle exercises are used or when lighter loads  (30-60% of  1 RM) are employed in explosive exercises (eg., pulls, cleans)  (73). Newton et al  (73) demonstrated that in a bench press exercise power output was not maximal over about 50% of the range of motion when 30% of the 1 RM  was lifted as explosively as possible. Activation of the antagonist muscles of the upper back and lack of neural activation in the chest over much of the range of motion to protect the joints may explain this observation. When the weight was allowed to be released from the hands at the end of the range of motion with use of a specialized testing device, power output was enhanced throughout the range of motion. Such data demonstrate why  "speed reps" may be counterproductive to power development in some exercises (eg., bench press, shoulder press)  and support the use of resistance training tools which allow one to release the weight  (eg., medicine ball) or use exercises where momentum is not a problem with the apparatus (eg., isokinetics, pneumatics, hydraulics, etc.) .

The ability  to exert force earlier in a movement plays a vital role in many athletic and everyday activities.

The development of power may be one of the most important physiological adaptations with  resistance exercise training. The ability to exert force earlier in a movement plays a vital role in many athletic and everyday activities. From an athletic perspective, it may be more appropriate to think of strength as the force capability of the muscle for actions ranging from the fastest eccentric to the fastest concentric actions. The forcevelocity relationship for muscle predicts that the faster the velocity of concentric muscle action, the lower the force that can be produced, yet maximal power is produced at intermediate velocities of movement ( a p proximately 30% of maximum shortening velocity). High force, not high power, is required in the sport of power lifting  (perhaps more accurately this sport should be called force lifting). If  an athlete is attempting to lift the maximum amount of weight, movement velocities are slightly higher than zero. Thus, one exhibits maximal force but very low levels of power during 1  R M   lifts.

Many strength and conditioning specialists believe that if slow velocity strength increases, then power output and dynamic performance will also improve. This is true to a certain extent because maximum strength, even at slow velocities, is a contributing factor to explosive power. All  explosive movements start from zero or slow velocities and it is at these phases of the movement that slow velocity strength can contribute to power development. However, as the muscles begin to achieve high velocities of shortening, slow velocity strength capacity has a reduced impact on the ability to produce high force at rapid shortening velocities. This fact becomes increasingly important as the athlete attempts to specifically train for optimal power development

Heavy resistance strength training using high resistance and slow velocities of concentric muscle action leads primarily to improvements in maximal strength  (ie., the high force/low velocity portion of the force-velocity curve), and improvements are reduced at higher velocities. Newton and Kraemer (72) proposed a "mixed-model" concept to o p timize each aspect of power develop ment (ie., power, coordination, strength, and muscle size). The use of slow velocity, heavy resistance training for the development of explosive power is often  justified  based on the fact that power is equal to force times the velocity of the muscle action. Thus, if  1 RM strength is increased, then this is all that is required from a resistance training proffam.  However, if power performance is to be maximized, then both the force and velocity c o m p nents must be trained. Because the movement distance is usually fixed by the specific  joint  range of motion, velocity is determined by  the time required to complete the movement. Therefore, training methods which decrease the time over which the movement is produced result in an increased power output. Intimately linked to this concept is the rate of force development. Specifically, explos i v e - ~ resistance training increases the slope of the early portion  of the force-time curve. Although heavy resistance training increases maximum strength and, thus, the highest point of the force-time curve, this type of training does not improve power performance appreciably, especially in athletes who have already developed a strength training base  (ie., more than 6 months of training). This is because the movement time during explosive activities is typically less than  300 msec, and most of the maximal force increases cannot be realized over such a short period of time. The athletes do not have the time to utilize their developed slow velocity strength.

In order to compare the effects of different resistance training modes on vertical  jump  performance, Wilson et al  (97) had subjects train for 10 weeks using either traditional back squats, loaded jump squats, or plyometric or stretch-shortening cycle training (drop  jumps). The loaded jump squats were performed using a resistance of 30% of maximum  to maximize mechanical  power output. All  the training groups showed increases in vertical  jump performance; however, the loaded jump squats group showed significantly greater increases  (18%) than the other two groups (5-10%). Berger  (7) also observed superior improvements in vertical jump ability in a group who performed jump squats with a resistance equal to 30% of maximum compared with groups that performed traditional weight training, plyometric training, and isometric training.

## CONCLUSION

Designing resistance exercise training programs requires an understanding of basic scientific principles. With the rapid improvements in knowledge pertaining to the physiological response to overload training, further developments are expected to enhance preparation in elite athletes. Although strength training has been shown to improve sporting performance as a result of an increase in size, strength, power, speed, flexibility, or agility, it is simplistic to believe that resistance exercise training alone will  bring about an improvement in athletic performance.  For this to occur, resistance exercises must be adapted to simulate the specific athletic conditions  (ie., movement, velocity, energy system, muscle groups, etc.).  If  the training is rigorously controlled  and monitored, the chance of injury is  minimal. Certainly, over the next decade, resistance exercise training will  become an integral tool not only for elite athletes but also for the recreational or novice athlete. JOSPT
